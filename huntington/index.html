Deconstructing Huntington

The "Clash of Civilizations" was not a historical discovery. It was a political projection — and Bulliet's evidence dismantles it structurally.

Primary Sources & Scholarship Samuel P. Huntington, The Clash of Civilizations and the Remaking of World Order (1996); Richard W. Bulliet, The Case for Islamo-Christian Civilization (Columbia, 2004); Marshall G.S. Hodgson, The Venture of Islam, 3 vols. (Chicago, 1974); Edward Said, Orientalism (1978); Amartya Sen, Identity and Violence (2006).

What Huntington Actually Argued

In a 1993 Foreign Affairs article and the 1996 book that followed, Samuel Huntington proposed that the primary axis of post-Cold War conflict would be cultural and civilizational — with the "Islamic civilization" and the "Western civilization" as the central antagonists.

Huntington's thesis rested on several empirical claims: that civilizations are defined by religion and culture rather than politics or economics; that Islamic and Western civilizations are fundamentally incompatible; that demographic growth in Muslim-majority countries creates inherent pressure against the West; and that the Cold War's end revealed civilizational fault lines that ideology had temporarily obscured.

The Structural Problem with Huntington

Huntington treats civilizations as bounded, self-contained units with fixed identities. This is historically false. Every civilization documented in this archive is a synthesis — not a monoculture. The "Islamic civilization" he identifies was founded by a Persian-Syriac-Arab multi-confessional consortium. The "Western civilization" he defends received its intellectual foundations through Arabic translation.

The Clash thesis requires the erasure of the Toledo Translations. It requires the erasure of Gondishapur. It requires the erasure of Hunayn ibn Ishaq. Without these erasures, the thesis cannot stand.

Bulliet's Counter-Argument: Sibling Civilizations

Richard Bulliet's The Case for Islamo-Christian Civilization (2004) is the most structurally rigorous response to Huntington. His argument is not rhetorical — it is empirical, based on comparative developmental analysis across twelve centuries.

Bulliet's central claim: Islam and the West are not alien civilizations. They are sibling civilizations — both descended from the same Axial Age inheritance (Hellenic philosophy, Abrahamic ethics, Roman-Byzantine administrative tradition), and both experiencing structurally parallel developmental stages:

Bulliet's Parallel Developmental Stages

Feudalism: Both civilizations experienced fragmentary local authority and agrarian economic organization in comparable periods (European feudalism: 9th–13th c.; Islamic fragmentation: 10th–13th c.).

Reform Movements: The Protestant Reformation (16th c.) and the Islamic reform movements (Wahhabism, Salafism, 18th–19th c.) are structurally parallel responses to perceived institutional corruption of a shared founding faith-and-reason synthesis.

Religious Wars: The European Wars of Religion (1524–1648 CE) find their structural parallel in the Sunni-Shia and intra-Islamic conflicts of the same period. These are not alien phenomena — they are the same civilizational pathology expressing itself in siblings.

Secularization pressure: Both civilizations are experiencing the tension between religious authority and state sovereignty in comparable contemporary terms. The European resolution (separation of church and state) is one historical outcome — not a universal law.

Hodgson's "Islamdom": The Alternative Framework

Marshall Hodgson's The Venture of Islam — three volumes produced at the University of Chicago in 1974 — offers the most sophisticated alternative to both Huntington's reductionism and naive universalism.

Hodgson insisted on precise vocabulary. He distinguished: "Islam" (the religion); "Islamic" (pertaining to that religion); "Islamicate" (the broader cultural sphere influenced by Islam but not religiously defined); and "Islamdom" (the world-system of interconnected societies where Islam was the primary cultural reference point).

Hodgson — The Venture of Islam
"The 'Islamicate' civilization was at its creative best a world civilization — not a sectarian one. The Persianate cultural synthesis that carried it was the product of a deliberate cosmopolitanism, in which the 'Urph (custom and convention) of diverse peoples was recognized as legitimate alongside the Shari'a."
Marshall G.S. Hodgson, The Venture of Islam, Vol. 2, University of Chicago Press, 1974

The concept of Urph — customary practice recognized as valid alongside religious law — is Hodgson's key to understanding how the "Islamicate" world synthesized Persian, Greek, Indian, and local cultural traditions without erasing them. This is the opposite of Huntington's civilizational essentialism.

The Evidence That Kills the Thesis

Beyond Bulliet and Hodgson, the archival evidence documented across this site constitutes a definitive empirical refutation of the Clash thesis:

The Four Disqualifying Facts

1. Gondishapur (3rd–7th c.): Greek philosophers exiled from Athens by a Christian emperor were given sanctuary by a Persian Zoroastrian king — creating the synthesis that the Abbasids later institutionalized. No civilization was "clashing" here.

2. The Baghdad Translation Court (8th–10th c.): Muslim caliphs funded Christian, Jewish, Sabian, and Persian scholars to translate and expand the Greek corpus. No clash; deliberate synthesis.

3. Toledo (12th–13th c.): Christian scholars came specifically to learn from Arabic texts and the scholars who had produced them. The Crusades were happening simultaneously. Both were true at once.

4. Averroes and Aquinas (13th c.): The architect of Catholic orthodoxy built his theology on the framework of a Moroccan Muslim philosopher. This is not a clash. This is a conversation that one side later stopped acknowledging.
