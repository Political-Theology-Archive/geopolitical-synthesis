The Report — Citation, Scope, and Primary Passages

Official Citation

Report of the Court of Inquiry Constituted under Punjab Act II of 1954 to Enquire into the Punjab Disturbances of 1953. Superintendent, Government Printing, Punjab, Lahore. Issued April 10, 1954. 387 pages. 117 sittings. Proceedings: July 1, 1953 – February 28, 1954. Full text: archive.org

The court was formally tasked with examining the circumstances leading to martial law, responsibility for the disturbances, and the adequacy of authorities' response. The Report ranged dramatically beyond these narrow terms — scholar Ali Usman Qasmi describes it as "an elaborate meditation on the evolution of Muslim nationalism and 'modernist Islam' in South Asia." The Report devoted major sections to: "An Islamic State," "Foundations of an Islamic State," and "Essentials of an Islamic State" — far exceeding its legal mandate. This expansion of scope into constitutional treatise was not incidental; it was the Report's political project.

The Report's most celebrated operation involved cross-examination of 117 Islamic scholars representing all Pakistani Muslim sects, asking each to provide a legally enforceable definition of "Muslim." The results were, by the Report's own account, a catalogue of irreconcilable contradiction. Each alim's definition, applied consistently, rendered followers of all other schools as kafir.

The Epistemological Trap — Three Moves of Colonial Capture

The "who is a Muslim" exercise was not a neutral inquiry. It was an epistemological trap structured by three deliberate moves — a pattern of colonial intellectual capture identifiable across multiple British imperial contexts.

The Three-Move Colonial Epistemological Trap
Move 1 — Category Imposition

The Report demanded a "definition of Muslim" in legal-positivist terms — judicially enforceable, mutually agreed, universally applicable. Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) never possessed such a definition because it was never designed for state enforcement. The tradition guides individual conscience and community practice; it was not built to produce the kind of uniform, legally operative taxonomy that European positivist law requires. Asking ulema to translate a living theological category into a positivist legal instrument is asking them to perform a task for which their tradition has no procedure — by design, and for good reason.

Move 2 — Pluralism Reframed as Failure

The Islamic tradition's internal pluralism — multiple valid schools of jurisprudence, centuries of productive theological disagreement, the existence of ikhtilaf (legitimate scholarly difference) as a feature of the tradition — is the normal, healthy functioning of a living intellectual system. The Report treated this pluralism as incompetence: "hopelessly disagreed among themselves." It applied a European positivist standard to measure what was never designed to meet that standard, then declared the tradition's principled pluralism as evidence of its unfitness for governance.

Move 3 — Colonial Substitution

Having manufactured the "failure" of Islamic self-definition through moves 1 and 2, the Report positioned the colonial Westminster constitutional framework — Jinnah's August 11, 1947 speech, Ivor Jennings' constitutional theory, the common law tradition — as the only available rational alternative. The colonial legal infrastructure is thereby presented as the neutral, modern, workable option in contrast to Islamic governance's demonstrated impossibility. The indigenous framework fails; the colonial framework saves.

This three-move structure is characteristic of British colonial epistemological capture: Render the indigenous intellectual framework as internally incoherent → use as evidence it cannot govern → substitute the colonial framework as neutral/rational. The same moves were applied to Hindu legal codes in Bengal, African customary law, and Chinese administrative tradition wherever British administration needed to delegitimize indigenous governance claims and justify the colonial legal replacement.

The Report's logical leap — from "ulema disagree" to "therefore secular state" — erases a third option the Report never acknowledges: a state that governs on Islamic civilizational principles through democratic ijtihad, without making any single ulema faction's theology the law of the land. This suppressed third option is Iqbal's vision, analyzed in Part VIII.
