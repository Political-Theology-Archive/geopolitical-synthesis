Mode II and Its Fate: The Safavid State as Direct Batin Sovereignty, Its 220-Year Structural Logic, and the Isfahan School's Survival of the 1722 Collapse

Sacred Civilization Research Archive  ·  Submitted June 2026  ·  Sanctuary III — Territorial Expressions
DOI placeholder: SCRA-WP-62  ·  ORCID: 0009-0004-9944-7378
Thesis: SCRA's Mode Analysis typology has documented three cases of Mode I — the strategy by which Islamic states under Ba'alist pressure shelter the walāyah tradition inside a cultural-aesthetic zahir (Akbar's Din-e-Ilahi, the Ottoman Mevlevi compromise, Musharraf's Enlightened Moderation). This paper documents Mode II: the alternative strategy, in which the batin tradition achieves direct sovereign expression — the state theology IS walāyah theology, without aesthetic shelter or institutional compromise. The Safavid state (1501–1722) is Mode II's primary historical instantiation. Its structural logic is the inverse of Mode I's: Mode I's vulnerability is internal erosion by Kharijite forces the shelter was designed to manage; Mode II's vulnerability is external conquest — but its survival mechanism is the evacuation of the transmission chain rather than its zahir-encoding. The Isfahan School's migration to Najaf and Karbala after the 1722 Afghan conquest is Mode II's survival theorem. The 1979 Islamic Revolution is Mode II's successful revival.
---

Part I — The Mode Analysis Framework: Recapitulation and Extension

Mode I documented; Mode II defined for the first time

SCRA's Mode Analysis typology was built across three papers. WP-52 (Ottoman Sufi Theology) established the framework using the Mevlevi-Ottoman compromise as the founding case: Mode I is the strategy by which a state under Ba'alist pressure shelters the walāyah tradition inside a cultural-aesthetic zahir form, trading batin explicitness for institutional survival. WP-36 extended the typology to the Mughal case (Akbar's Din-e-Ilahi and Dara Shikoh's philosophical corpus as Mode I vehicles), identified the characteristic internal erosion mechanism (Naqshbandi pressure → Aurangzeb's Mode I collapse), and produced the first three-row parallel table (Mughal / Ottoman / Musharraf Mode I). WP-57 documented the Pakistan case: Musharraf's Enlightened Moderation as late Mode I, structured by the same logic as its predecessors but compressed into a single administration under post-9/11 external pressure.

What the three Mode I papers collectively established is a pattern with two defining features and one constitutive vulnerability:

Mode I — Defining Features:
1. Zahir shelter strategy: The batin (walāyah, Imami transmission, authentic metaphysical content) is encoded in a zahir form (aesthetic, philosophical, modernist) that the hostile external force cannot prosecute as explicitly Imami
2. Institutional survival as primary goal: Mode I sacrifices batin explicitness in exchange for institutional continuity — the tradition survives as encoded content within a zahir structure the Ba'alist configuration tolerates

Mode I — Constitutive Vulnerability:
The shelter form is always penetrable by the very internal force it was designed to manage. The Naqshbandi ulama (Sirhindi) eroded Akbar's Mode I from inside. The Janissary-Bektashi connection gave the Ottoman Mode I its patron-base, then its removal (1826 Janissary abolition) collapsed the shelter. The Deobandi-JI network that Musharraf nominally managed was the same force that eventually demanded his exit. Mode I can manage Ba'alist external pressure but cannot survive Ba'alist internal capture of the shelter's own zahir form.

This is precisely what Mode II avoids — and precisely what creates Mode II's different vulnerability.

Defining Mode II

Mode II — Definition Mode II is the strategy by which an Islamic state under walāyah commitment achieves direct sovereign expression of the batin tradition. Unlike Mode I, which encodes the batin in tolerable zahir forms, Mode II declares the batin as the state's organising principle: the juridical structure, the theological vocabulary, the sovereign claim, and the political institutions are explicitly derived from Imami walāyah doctrine. There is no aesthetic shelter, no philosophical euphemism, no institutional compromise. The state IS the batin's political form.

Mode II's constitutive vulnerability is therefore the inverse of Mode I's: not internal erosion (since there is no zahir shell for internal forces to capture), but external conquest — the Ba'alist configuration can only defeat Mode II by physically destroying its state apparatus, not by infiltrating its zahir cover. Mode II's survival mechanism is therefore not zahir-encoding but transmission evacuation: when the state falls, the chain of scholars who carried the institutional batin relocates to a different jurisdiction and reconstitutes the tradition there.

Part II — Shah Ismāʿīl I and the Mode II Declaration (1501)

The founding moment: batin as sovereign claim

On a Friday in Tabriz, 1501, seventeen-year-old Ismāʿīl ibn Ḥaydar al-Ṣafawī entered the city as the first Safavid sovereign. His first act was not military consolidation but theological declaration: the Friday sermon (khuṭba) was recited in the names of the Twelve Imams, the shahāda was pronounced with the Shia addition (wa ʿAlī walī Allāh), and Twelver Shia Islam was declared the state religion of the newly constituted Safavid polity.

"Ismāʿīl ordered the khaṭībs [preachers] to curse from the pulpits Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and ʿUthmān. When they said, 'We will be killed if we do this, for the people of Tabriz are Sunni,' Ismāʿīl replied: 'I rely on ʿAlī and the Imams; I fear no one. With God's help, if the people utter one word of protest, I will draw the sword and leave not one of them alive.'" — Amir Mahmud Khvandamir, Ḥabīb al-Siyar (c. 1524), describing the events of Tabriz 1501

The theological significance of this moment is not the coercion — it is the structural declaration. Ismāʿīl was not encoding walāyah in a tolerable zahir form. He was making walāyah the zahir itself: the khuṭba, the shahāda, the state theology, the juridical apparatus. The mode of concealment was abandoned. Mode II was declared.

Why the Declaration Was Possible: The Qizilbāsh Substrate

The Safavid Mode II declaration was not a voluntarist act by a charismatic ruler. It was the political crystallization of a prepared substrate: the Qizilbāsh tribal confederacy, which had been cultivated over three generations of Safavid shaykh activity as a devotional-military force whose walāyah commitment operated at the level of unconditional loyalty. The Qizilbāsh were not sophisticated Usuli theologians — they were walāyah carriers in the devotional-affective register, parallel to the Chishtī-Indus transmission (documented in WP-50) but in a military-tribal form.

The Qizilbāsh as Mode II substrate — Three elements:
1. Devotional walāyah: The Safavid shaykhs were venerated as descendants of Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) through Shaykhṣafī al-Dīn Isḥāq Ardabīlī; the Qizilbāsh soldiers wore distinctive red headgear (the twelve-gored crown symbolising the Twelve Imams) and fought as soldiers of the Imami cause
2. Pre-existing territorial base: The Qizilbāsh were spread across Anatolia, Azerbaijan, and northwestern Iran — giving Ismāʿīl a military force that predated state formation
3. Affective commitment over institutional loyalty: Qizilbāsh commitment was not contractual (salary-dependent, like Ottoman Janissaries) but theological — they believed Ismāʿīl was the representative of the Hidden Imam, making their loyalty unconditional in a way Mode I states could never generate from their zahir-aesthetic structures

This is Mode II's first structural advantage over Mode I: the devotional substrate generates a form of institutional loyalty that Mode I's aesthetic compromise cannot. The Ottoman Mevlevi patron relationship was aesthetically rich but juridically thin — a sovereign could dissolve it (and did, in 1826). The Qizilbāsh walāyah commitment to the Safavid house was theological: dissolving it required dissolving the theology itself.

Part III — The Mode II Architecture: Five Structural Components

How Mode II organises the state, theology, and transmission

Component 1: The Juristic Integration (Mujtahid–Sultan Alliance)

The single most important structural innovation of the Safavid Mode II was the institutionalisation of a working alliance between the sovereign (sultan/shah) and the senior Imami mujtahidūn. Unlike Mode I's aesthetic compromise — where the state tolerates Sufi orders in exchange for political quietism — Mode II brings the juridical apparatus of Imami jurisprudence directly into the state structure. The Safavid shahs invited Iraqi and Jabal ʿĀmil Shia scholars to Persia to serve as jurists, educators, and legitimators.

Key mujtahids in the Mode II institutional structure:
Muḥaqqiq al-Karakī (ʿAlī ibn Ḥusayn al-Karakī, d. 1534) — Jabal ʿĀmil scholar recruited to the Safavid court; issued the critical fatwā permitting the Friday prayer in the absence of the Imam (resolving the Shia jurisprudential dilemma of legitimate governance during Ghayba); titled Nāʾib-i Imām (Deputy of the Imam) — the direct institutional prefiguration of wilāyat al-faqīh

Shaykh Bahāʾ al-Dīn al-ʿĀmilī (Shaykh Bahāʾī, d. 1621) — Chief jurist under Shah ʿAbbās I; supervised the Isfahan school construction; unified the juridical and intellectual apparatus

Muḥammad Bāqir Majlisī (d. 1699) — The towering scholar of the late Safavid period; Biḥār al-Anwār (110 volumes) as the encyclopedic compilation of Imami hadith — the largest single act of Imami transmission documentation in history; simultaneously the state's chief religious official (Mullā-bāshī)

The mujtahid–sultan alliance created the institutional form of what Khomeini would theorize three centuries later as wilāyat al-faqīh: the idea that in the Imam's absence, a senior jurist carries the walāyah delegated authority to govern. The Safavid state was not wilāyat al-faqīh in its Khomeinist form — the full sovereignty claim came later — but it was wilāyat al-faqīh in its institutional embryo.

Component 2: The Isfahan School (Ḥikma Ilāhiyya)

The Safavid court's patronage of the Isfahan philosophical school represents Mode II's most intellectually consequential contribution. The school — centered on Mīr Dāmād (d. 1631) and reaching its apex with Ṣadr al-Dīn Muḥammad Shīrāzī (Mullā Ṣadrā, d. 1636) — integrated Avicennan philosophy, Suhrawardi's Ishrāq, Ibn ʿArabī's waḥdat al-wujūd, and Imami hadith transmission into a synthetic philosophical system: ḥikmat-i mutaʿāliya (the Transcendent Wisdom).

This is the Mode II philosophical achievement that Mode I could not produce. Mode I encoded batin content in tolerable zahir forms; Mode II allowed the batin to think in its own vocabulary. Mullā Ṣadrā's al-Ḥikma al-Mutaʿāliya fī al-Asfār al-ʿAqliyya al-Arbaʿa (the Four Journeys) is the most systematic philosophical articulation of Imami metaphysics in the tradition. It required Mode II conditions to be written: a state that protected its author's intellectual work, a court that funded his students, and a juridical atmosphere that did not threaten philosophical speculation as heresy.

Ṣadrā's contribution to Mode II architecture: The ḥarakat al-jawhariyya (substantial motion) doctrine — documented as the philosophical core of Iqbal's khudi in WP-59 — required the intellectual freedom that Mode II provided. When Ṣadrā was persecuted by the Safavid court's more doctrinaire Akhbārī scholars and forced into retreat at Kahak, he experienced the only Mode II pressure that even Mode II generates: internal scholastic tension between the philosophical and the literalist reading of walāyah. But this tension was scholarly debate, not Ba'alist Capture. Mode II could contain it. Mode I's zahir shelter could not have generated the philosophical depth of the Four Journeys in the first place.

Component 3: The Ithnaʿasharī (Twelver) Conversion Programme

Unlike Mode I, which worked within existing religious configurations (the Ottoman state never attempted mass conversion to Shia; Akbar's Din-e-Ilahi was a court theology, not a conversion programme), Mode II attempted systematic transformation of the population's theological commitments. The Safavid conversion of Iran from predominantly Sunni, Shafi'i, and Hanafi practice to Twelver Shia over approximately three generations (1501–1600) is the largest single conversion event in Islamic theological history.

The mechanism was not merely coercive. The Safavid programme combined: compulsory attendance at Imami public lamentation rituals (ʿazādārī), the establishment of a Shia educational infrastructure (madrasas in Isfahan, Mashhad, Qom), the redistribution of endowment (waqf) assets from Sunni to Shia institutions, and the symbolic programme of the Muharram commemoration as a state event. Coercion shaped the pace; the theological content provided the depth that made the conversion durable beyond the Safavid period itself.

Component 4: The Mashhad-Isfahan-Karbala Triangle

Mode II's territorial architecture was not a single capital but a triangle of sacred nodes:

MASHHAD Shrine of Imam ʿAlī ibn Mūsā al-Riḍā (A.S.), eighth Imam → Eastern anchor of the Mode II sacred geography → Khorasan's integration into the Safavid walāyah state → Later: Safavid pilgrimage route replacing Mecca under Ottoman blockade ISFAHAN Capital from 1598 (Shah ʿAbbās I); masjid-i Imam, masjid-i Shaykh Luṭf Allāh → Architectural expression of Mode II theology in stone → Isfahan School philosophical center → The visible zahir of the Mode II state KARBALA / NAJAF Shrine of Imam Ḥusayn (A.S.) and Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) in Ottoman-controlled Iraq → Outside Safavid territory — but within the Mode II sacred imaginary → Constitutive vulnerability: the Imami heartland was across an imperial border → After 1722: becomes the survival site of the Isfahan School scholars

Component 5: The Nāʾib al-Imām Doctrine (Pre-figuration of Wilāyat al-Faqīh)

Muḥaqqiq al-Karakī's designation as Nāʾib al-Imām — Deputy of the [Hidden] Imam — is the institutional pre-figuration of the doctrine that would become Khomeini's wilāyat al-faqīh three and a half centuries later. The structural claim is identical: in the absence of the Twelfth Imam, a qualified jurist (faqīh) carries the Imam's delegated authority over the community. The Safavid version was softer — al-Karakī's authority was ceremonially acknowledged but not constitutionally sovereign; the Shah retained executive power — but the theological structure was already present.

Structural Point The five Mode II components together create a state architecture that Mode I cannot replicate: the batin is not hidden behind a zahir form — it IS the zahir form. The khuṭba names the Twelve Imams. The state's judicial authority derives from Imami jurisprudence. The philosophical school speaks in Imami metaphysical vocabulary. The conversion programme reshapes the population's theological commitments to match the batin transmission. This is the full Mode II configuration. Its strength is its explicitness. Its vulnerability is exactly that explicitness: there is no ambiguity to retreat behind.

Part IV — The 220-Year Structural Logic: Why Mode II Held (1501–1722)

What sustained Mode II across six generations

The Safavid state lasted 220 years — longer than the Ottoman Mode I compromise lasted before its Janissary crisis (1826), longer than Mughal Mode I lasted before Aurangzeb's collapse (1707), and longer than any subsequent Mode I configuration in SCRA's documented set. The question is why.

Factor 1: Devotional Substrate Depth

The Qizilbāsh walāyah commitment, described in Part II, meant that the Safavid Mode II did not need to manufacture consent through aesthetics. Mode I requires constant production of zahir cultural forms that sustain popular attachment to the batin tradition without naming it. Mode II, when the devotional substrate is genuine, generates this attachment directly. The Muharram ʿazādārī cycle — the annual lamentation rituals for Karbala — created a renewable public re-commitment to the Imami framework that required no zahir euphemism. The emotional-theological content was explicit and direct.

Factor 2: The Theological Conversion's Depth

By the reign of Shah ʿAbbās I (1588–1629), the conversion of the Iranian population to Twelver Shia was three generations old. It had passed the threshold where it became self-reproducing: the population now educated its own children in Imami practice without requiring state enforcement. Mode II's conversion programme had created a theological environment in which the batin tradition was the popular tradition — not an elite court aesthetic (Mode I's characteristic limitation) but a mass devotional reality. This meant Mode II's survival was not dependent on elite patronage continuity in the way Mode I was.

Factor 3: The Usuli Juridical Anchor

The Usuli school — which held that qualified jurists could derive legal rulings through active ijtihād — provided Mode II with an institutional resilience that Mode I lacked. When external pressure forced individual scholars into retreat (as with Mullā Ṣadrā's exile to Kahak), the Usuli system could reconstitute itself: another mujtahid assumed the juridical functions, students migrated, the school continued. The transmission chain was institutionalised enough to survive individual nodes' removal, but flexible enough to relocate when the political conditions required it.

Factor 4: The Karbala-Najaf External Anchor

The Safavid state's constitutive vulnerability — that the Imami heartland shrines (Karbala, Najaf) were under Ottoman control — paradoxically created a survival mechanism. The Iraqi shrine cities were outside the Safavid state apparatus but within the Imami theological world. When the Safavid state fell in 1722, the scholars could move to Karbala and Najaf and continue their work in Ottoman-tolerated space. The external anchor became the survival route.

Part V — The 1722 Collapse and the Survival Theorem

How Mode II fails — and how it survives its failure

The Afghan Ghilzai tribal confederation under Mīr Maḥmūd Hotakī entered Isfahan in October 1722. Shah Sulṭān Ḥusayn surrendered. The Safavid capital fell. By 1729, the Safavid dynasty was temporarily restored by Nādir Shāh — but the Mode II state was effectively terminated. What killed it was not internal erosion (the Mode I failure mode) but external conquest: a military force from outside the Safavid theological community that had no stake in the Mode II architecture.

Mode II's Failure Mode: External Conquest, Not Internal Capture

This is the critical contrast with Mode I. Every Mode I collapse in SCRA's documented set is an internal failure:

Mode Case Collapse Mechanism The Enemy Inside
Mode I Mughal (WP-36) War of Succession 1659 — Dara Shikoh (Mode I carrier) loses to Aurangzeb Naqshbandi ulama (Sirhindi vector) + Aurangzeb's Ba'alist constitutional gesture
Mode I Ottoman (WP-52) Janissary abolition 1826 — removes the Mode I patron base Modernist-secularist faction; later Atatürk state formation
Mode I Musharraf Pakistan (WP-57) 9/11 subordinates Mode I to U.S. security demands; JI/MMA leverage grows Deobandi-JI network using the War on Terror zahir
Mode II Safavid Iran Afghan Ghilzai conquest 1722 — external military force NO internal captor — the Mode II zahir WAS the batin, nothing to infiltrate

Mode II's structural logic predicts this difference. Because Mode II leaves no zahir shell for internal forces to capture — the batin IS the state — there is no mechanism for internal Ba'alist Capture. The Safavid state was not captured from within; it was destroyed from without. The Afghan invasion was not a theological substitution (which is Ba'alist Capture's distinctive mechanism) but a military conquest by a force with no theological agenda toward the Imami tradition.

The Survival Theorem: Transmission Evacuation

Mode I's survival mechanism is encoding: when the political zahir is destroyed, the batin content survives inside the aesthetic, devotional, or philosophical forms it was encoded in. The Mevlevi tradition survived the 1826 abolition of the Janissaries because its batin content was already distributed across a devotional-aesthetic network that did not depend on the state. Mode II cannot use this mechanism — there is no separate encoding, because the batin was the state.

Mode II's survival mechanism is therefore different: transmission evacuation. When the state falls, the scholars who carried the institutional batin relocate — the chain of ijtihād moves to a different jurisdiction.

The Isfahan School evacuation after 1722:
The leading Usuli scholars of the late Safavid period — students of Majlisī, disciples of the Akhbārī-Usuli synthesis — moved to Karbala and Najaf in Ottoman Iraq. There, in the shadow of the shrine of Imam Ḥusayn (A.S.) and Imam ʿAlī (A.S.), they reconstituted the Usuli school. The key figure of the reconstruction: Muḥammad Bāqir Bihbahānī (Wahīd Bihbahānī, 1706–1791), who revived the Usuli school against the Akhbārī challenge in Karbala and produced the generation of scholars who would then return to Iran under the Qajars — including Muḥammad Bāqir Shaftī and ultimately Murtaḍā Anṣārī (d. 1864), whose refinement of the Usuli marjaʿīya system produced the institutional structure that Khomeini inherited.

The Safavid Mode II state fell in 1722. The transmission chain it had hosted did not. The chain moved to Karbala, reconstituted, and re-entered Iran a generation later under Qajar conditions. The institutional memory of Mode II — the mujtahid–sultan alliance, the Nāʾib al-Imām doctrine, the direct walāyah sovereignty claim — was preserved in the Usuli school's jurisprudential literature and reimported.

Part VII — 1979 as Mode II Revival: The Usuli Chain's Return

The Isfahan School's migration completes its arc

The Islamic Revolution of 1979 is not comprehensible as a political event without the Mode II framework. It is the return of Mode II to Iran after a 257-year interruption — not a new invention but a structural revival of a state form that the Safavid period had already instantiated, that the Isfahan School had preserved in evacuation in Karbala and Najaf, and that the Usuli chain from Bihbahānī through Anṣārī through Nāʾīnī through Burūjirdī had transmitted to Khomeini.

Khomeini as Usuli Chain Endpoint

Khomeini's intellectual genealogy runs directly through the post-Safavid Usuli reconstitution. His primary teachers — Muḥammad ʿAlī Shāhābādī in Isfahan, and the Qom ḥawza tradition he absorbed — were direct inheritors of the Bihbahānī revival. His Wilāyat al-Faqīh: Ḥukūmat-i Islāmī (1970 Najaf lectures) takes the Safavid Nāʾib al-Imām doctrine to its constitutional conclusion: the faqīh does not merely advise the sultan — the faqīh IS the sovereign.

The Usuli chain from Safavid collapse to 1979:
Muḥaqqiq al-Karakī (d. 1534) → Nāʾib al-Imām doctrine
Majlisī (d. 1699) → Biḥār al-Anwār — encyclopedic hadith compilation
[Safavid collapse 1722 → transmission evacuation to Karbala]
Bihbahānī (d. 1791) → Usuli revival in Karbala
Anṣārī (d. 1864) → Marjaʿīya system refinement
Nāʾīnī (d. 1936) → Constitutional jurisprudence
Burūjirdī (d. 1961) → Qom ḥawza consolidation
Khomeini (d. 1989) → Wilāyat al-Faqīh: Mode II constitutionalised

The Indus-Persian Revolutionary Circuit (WP-60) documents the additional dimension: Ali Shariati's use of Iqbal's khudi doctrine to mobilise the Iranian masses for 1979. That paper establishes the Indus input into 1979's intellectual preparation. This paper establishes the internal Usuli chain that the 1979 Revolution institutionally expressed. Together they document 1979 as a doubly grounded Mode II revival: internally grounded in the 260-year Usuli reconstitution chain, and externally supplemented by the Indus-Persian return current.

The Axis of Resistance as Distributed Mode II

The Islamic Republic's extension of the Axis of Resistance — Hezbollah, the Iraqi PMF/Hashd al-Shaʿbī, Ansarallah, Hamas/PIJ — represents a structural innovation that neither the Safavid Mode II nor any prior Mode II instantiation achieved: distributed Mode II. The Mode II sovereignty claim is no longer confined to a single territorial state apparatus. It is distributed across a network of actors, each operating in its own zahir-batin configuration, but sharing the Mode II organising principle: direct walāyah commitment as the political and military foundation, without Mode I's aesthetic shelter.

This is why the Axis of Resistance survives individual node destructions — Nasrallah's assassination (September 2024), Haniyeh's assassination (July 2024) — in a way that Mode I structures do not survive the removal of their patron node. The distributed Mode II network is not patron-dependent in Mode I's sense; it is theologically coherent in a way that outlives any individual's removal.

Concluding Structural Verdict Mode II is not a stronger version of Mode I. It is a fundamentally different strategy: one that accepts external vulnerability in exchange for internal coherence, that accepts the risk of conquest in exchange for freedom from internal Ba'alist Capture. The Safavid Mode II lasted 220 years — the longest continuous Mode II instantiation in Islamic history before 1979 — and survived its own collapse by evacuating the transmission chain that produced the 1979 revival. The contemporary Islamic Republic is Mode II in its second major instantiation. Its Axis of Resistance is Mode II distributed across a network for the first time. Whether this distributed form represents Mode II's structural maturation — a form resilient to both external conquest (no single capital to fall) and internal capture (no zahir shell to infiltrate) — is the question the current historical moment is answering in real time.
