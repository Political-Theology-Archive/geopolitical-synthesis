Abstract

The Quran documents the Banī Isrā'īl's departure from Hārūn — Mūsā's (A.S.) divinely designated wasī (authorized successor) — during Mūsā's forty-night absence to receive the Torah. The community bypassed the appointed wasī and followed Sāmirī's constructed substitute authority (the golden calf). This is not a story about idol-worship as personal sin. It is the Quran's documentation of the structural pattern by which a community diverts from its prophetically designated succession line, installs a constructed substitute, and initiates a civilizational diversion. The Quran records this pattern because it will recur.

Saqīfa — the gathering at which, following the Prophet Muḥammad's (ﷺ) death, the community bypassed Imam ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (A.S.) despite the explicit Ghadīr Khumm appointment — is the Hārūn pattern's recurrence within the Islamic community. The SCRA's central argument in this paper: this is not a Shia sectarian grievance. It is a Quranic structural analysis — the Quran forecast this event by documenting its structural template. Recognizing Saqīfa as a Quranic structural recurrence converts the question from "Shia versus Sunni" to "does the Hārūn pattern apply?" — a question resolvable by Quranic structural analysis rather than by sectarian preference.

Part I

The Nass Principle — Divine Appointment as the Furqān Criterion for Authority

The Furqān (f-r-q: to separate/distinguish cleanly) is the divine criterion that separates haqq from bāṭil without ambiguity. In the question of legitimate authority over the Islamic community — who has the right to govern, to interpret the revelation, to guide the Umma — the Furqān criterion is nass: divine appointment. Not consensus (ijmāʿ), not tribal seniority, not military achievement, not commercial standing. God appoints; His appointment is the only criterion that cuts through all human arguments about precedence.

The nass principle is not a Shia theological invention. It is the structural principle by which every prophetic succession was organized in the Quranic record. Ibrāhīm (A.S.) was appointed — not elected by the Chaldeans. Mūsā (A.S.) was appointed at the burning bush — not selected by the Banī Isrā'īl. Hārūn (A.S.) was appointed as Mūsā's wasī by God's explicit command. Dāwūd (A.S.) was appointed. Sulaymān (A.S.) inherited through divine appointment. ʿĪsā (A.S.) was born of miraculous divine action. The entire prophetic chain operates through nass — divine designation of the person through whom God's guidance flows to the community. Human consensus, where it exists, ratifies the divine appointment; it does not constitute it.

وَرَبُّكَ يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَاءُ وَيَخْتَارُ ۗ مَا كَانَ لَهُمُ الْخِيَرَةُ
"Your Lord creates what He wills and chooses — the choice is not theirs."
Quran 28:68 — the nass principle stated as absolute theological axiom

Q 28:68 is the Quranic statement of the nass principle at its most absolute: God creates and God chooses — the community has no independent right of selection in matters of prophetic and Imamic designation. This verse's placement in Sūrat al-Qaṣaṣ (the chapter of narratives) — which contains the detailed Mūsā-Fir'awn account — is not incidental. The Quranic structure places the nass principle immediately in the context of the prophetic narrative to establish: the same divine prerogative that appointed Mūsā (A.S.) to confront Fir'awn operates in all succession questions.

Furqān and Nass — The Connection The Furqān's function is to separate haqq from bāṭil without ambiguity. In the succession question, nass IS the Furqān: it provides an unambiguous divine criterion that cuts through all competing human claims. The community that accepts nass as its governing criterion for succession questions has a functioning Furqān. The community that replaces nass with human consensus (shūrā without divine direction, ijmāʿ without prophetic designation) has replaced the Furqān criterion with a human criterion — producing a succession structure that cannot be distinguished from Ba'alist authority on purely formal grounds, since Ba'alist authority also claims community legitimacy through power, consensus, and institutional precedent.
Part II

The Hārūn Pattern — Quranic Documentation

The Hārūn (A.S.) wasī appointment is documented in multiple Quranic passages. Its structural elements are precise and repeated, establishing that this is not incidental biographical detail but a theologically significant structural template:

وَقَالَ مُوسَىٰ لِأَخِيهِ هَارُونَ اخْلُفْنِي فِي قَوْمِي وَأَصْلِحْ وَلَا تَتَّبِعْ سَبِيلَ الْمُفْسِدِينَ
"And Mūsā said to his brother Hārūn: Be my successor (khalīfa) among my people, set things right, and do not follow the way of the corrupters."
Quran 7:142
وَلَقَدْ قَالَ لَهُمْ هَارُونُ مِن قَبْلُ يَا قَوْمِ إِنَّمَا فُتِنتُم بِهِ ۖ وَإِنَّ رَبَّكُمُ الرَّحْمَٰنُ فَاتَّبِعُونِي وَأَطِيعُوا أَمْرِي
"And Hārūn had already said to them before [the calf was worshipped]: O my people, you are only being tested by this. Your Lord is the Most Merciful, so follow me and obey my command."
Quran 20:90 — Hārūn's explicit appeal to the Banī Isrā'īl to follow the appointed wasī

The structural elements of the Hārūn appointment are:

The Wasī Pattern — Structural Elements

1. Divine authorization through the Prophet. Hārūn is appointed by Mūsā (A.S.) — himself divinely authorized — as the explicit successor (khulufnī: take my place, be my khalīfa) for the period of the Prophet's absence. The appointment is public, formal, and divinely sanctioned through the Prophet's authority. This is the nass structure: divine authorization flowing through the Prophet to the designated successor.

2. The Prophet's physical absence creates the succession test. Mūsā goes to Mount Sinai for forty nights. The test is not whether Hārūn is competent — it is whether the community will maintain the succession structure during the period when the Prophet is not physically present to enforce it. The Ba'alist opportunity always arises in the absence of the prophetic authority: can the community maintain the walāya-structure on the basis of conviction, or does it require the Prophet's physical presence as enforcement?

3. The appointed wasī explicitly calls the community to obedience. Q 20:90 documents Hārūn's appeal: follow me and obey my command. The wasī does not remain silent. He invokes his authority, identifies the test, and calls the community to maintain the succession structure. The community is not ignorant — it has been explicitly called to follow the appointed successor.

4. The community follows the constructed substitute anyway. The Banī Isrā'īl follow Sāmirī's golden calf despite Hārūn's explicit appeal. This is the definitive structural move: the community, presented with a choice between the divinely appointed succession structure (Hārūn) and a constructed substitute authority (the golden calf), chooses the constructed substitute. The constructed substitute is not more legitimate — it is more convenient, more immediately gratifying, more compatible with the community's existing attachments and power structures.

Part III

The Sāmirī Mechanism — How Constructed Substitute Authority Operates

Sāmirī is the Quran's archetypal figure of the constructed substitute authority — the agent who, in the absence of the prophetic authority, produces a counterfeit that the community accepts. Q 20:85-97 gives the most detailed account: Sāmirī took "a handful from the messenger's footprint" — the most charged formulation in the Quranic account — and cast it into the golden calf, producing a calf that could make a lowing sound.

قَالَ بَصُرْتُ بِمَا لَمْ يَبْصُرُوا بِهِ فَقَبَضْتُ قَبْضَةً مِّنْ أَثَرِ الرَّسُولِ فَنَبَذْتُهَا وَكَذَٰلِكَ سَوَّلَتْ لِي نَفْسِي
"He said: I saw what they did not see, so I took a handful from the messenger's footprint and cast it in — and thus my soul prompted me."
Quran 20:96 — Sāmirī's account of the construction of the substitute authority

The Shia exegetical tradition — particularly in the tafsīr of ʿAllāma Ṭabāṭabāʾī's Al-Mīzān and the narrations from Imam Bāqir (A.S.) and Imam Ṣādiq (A.S.) — identifies several structural dimensions of the Sāmirī mechanism that are directly applicable to the Saqīfa analysis:

A. The constructed substitute uses fragments of prophetic authenticity. Sāmirī's formulation — "a handful from the messenger's footprint" — indicates that the substitute authority is not pure fabrication; it incorporates elements of genuine prophetic tradition. The golden calf was not simply a Canaanite idol imported wholesale — it was constructed using material from the prophetic context, giving it a veneer of connection to authentic sources. The Saqīfa parallel: the constructed caliphate authority used the vocabulary, the forms, and many of the institutional structures of the prophetic community. It retained the zahir of Islam while severing the bāṭin succession line. This is the Ba'alist Capture pattern at the foundational Islamic succession moment: māhiyya (Islamic institutional forms) preserved; iḍāfa (the live succession line to the walāya-source) severed.

B. The constructed substitute is immediately gratifying. The golden calf's lowing sound — it makes a noise — provides the community with an immediate sensory confirmation that this object has divine energy. The Banī Isrā'īl could see and hear the calf; Hārūn required them to maintain conviction without Mūsā's physical presence. The constructed substitute authority at Saqīfa operated identically: it provided immediate political resolution to the succession crisis, clear leadership structures, and the comfort of social cohesion. Maintaining the nass-based succession to Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) required a level of conviction and principled commitment that the community — exhausted by the Prophet's death and anxious about the immediate political situation — could not sustain against the pressure of the Saqīfa gathering's momentum.

C. The wasī cannot physically prevent the deviation. Hārūn (A.S.) does not use force against the golden calf worshippers — as Mūsā (A.S.) demands to know upon his return (Q 7:150: why did you not fight them?). Hārūn's response is crucial: I feared you would say I caused division among the Banī Isrā'īl (Q 7:150). The appointed wasī, operating without the Prophet's physical authority, cannot impose the succession line through force without fragmenting the community itself — and fragmentation serves the Ba'alist opponent more than an imperfect unity. Imam ʿAlī's (A.S.) conduct at Saqīfa follows the same structural logic: he did not raise the sword to enforce his appointment, explicitly stating in Nahj al-Balāgha (Sermon 3, Khutba al-Shiqshiqiyya) that he restrained himself to avoid fragmenting the nascent Islamic community.

Part IV

Ghadīr Khumm — The Nass Event: Establishing the Prophetic Appointment

The Ghadīr Khumm event (18 Dhū al-Ḥijja 10 AH / March 632 CE) is the Muḥammadi (ﷺ) nass — the explicit divine appointment of Imam ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (A.S.) as the wasī and legitimate successor to the prophetic authority. The event occurred at the pool of Khumm on the return from the Farewell Ḥajj, with the Prophet stopping 100,000+ returning pilgrims — making this one of the most publicly witnessed events in early Islamic history.

مَنْ كُنْتُ مَوْلَاهُ فَهَذَا عَلِيٌّ مَوْلَاهُ ۚ اللَّهُمَّ وَالِ مَنْ وَالَاهُ ۚ وَعَادِ مَنْ عَادَاهُ
"Whoever I am their Mawlā (master/guardian/closest authority) — this ʿAlī is their Mawlā. O God, love those who love him and be an adversary to those who are adversarial to him."
Hadith al-Ghadīr — narrated by 110+ Companions; transmitted in both Shia and Sunni ḥadīth collections including Musnad Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal, Sunan Ibn Māja, and Tirmidhī

The Ghadīr declaration is the Islamic nass — structurally parallel to Mūsā's appointment of Hārūn (Q 7:142). The structural parallels are precise:

The cross-sectarian attestation of Ghadīr Khumm is extensive. The declaration is recorded in major Sunni ḥadīth collections — Musnad Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal, Sunan Ibn Māja, Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī — not as a disputed or weak narration but as an established historical event. The dispute is not whether it occurred but what mawlā means: Sunni scholarship generally reads it as "friend/supporter" (walī in the general sense); Shia scholarship reads it as "master/guardian/authority" (mawlā in the authority-conferring sense that makes the declaration a succession appointment). This paper argues that the Hārūn-pattern structural analysis resolves the disambiguation: in the context of the structural template (Prophet appointing wasī before an impending departure), the authority-conferring reading is the only reading consistent with the Quranic structural parallel.

Part V

Saqīfa — The Hārūn Pattern Applied

The Saqīfa Banī Sāʿida gathering occurred within days — some accounts say hours — of the Prophet's (ﷺ) death. The Anṣār (Medinese Muslims) gathered to select a successor from among themselves; Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and Abū ʿUbayda arrived; a complex negotiation followed; Abū Bakr was pledged allegiance. Imam ʿAlī (A.S.), occupied with the Prophet's burial preparations, was not present. By the time the burial was complete, the succession had been decided.

The Hārūn pattern structural elements are present with precise fidelity:

Structural Correspondence — Hārūn Pattern / Saqīfa Event

1. Divine authorization of the wasī is prior and explicit. Ghadīr Khumm is the nass — publicly witnessed, cross-sectarianly attested. The appointment precedes the succession crisis. The community that gathered at Saqīfa knew about Ghadīr Khumm. (Umar's congratulation to Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) at Ghadīr is documented — he did not dispute the event occurred; the dispute was what it meant.)

2. The Prophet's physical death creates the succession test. Exactly as Mūsā's absence to receive the Torah created the test for the Banī Isrāʾīl. The test is whether the community maintains the appointment structure in the absence of the Prophet's physical enforcement authority. Q 3:144 anticipates this precise moment: Muḥammad is but a messenger; messengers have passed before him. If he dies or is killed, will you turn on your heels?

3. The wasī does not use force to impose the appointment. Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) — like Hārūn (A.S.) — restrains himself. Nahj al-Balāgha Sermon 3 (Khutba al-Shiqshiqiyya) documents his self-restraint explicitly: he saw his right being taken, endured it, but chose not to fragment the nascent community by civil war. This is the Hārūn logic: the wasī's legitimacy does not require force — force would serve the Ba'alist disruption strategy better than patient endurance.

4. The constructed substitute authority uses fragments of authentic succession. The caliphate established at Saqīfa was not a pure fabrication — it used Islamic vocabulary, Islamic institutional structures, genuine commitment to the Prophet's sunnah (in most areas). It was a Sāmirī construction: the zahir of authentic succession with the bāṭin succession line severed. Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and ʿUthmān were not enemies of Islam — they were the Sāmirī-pattern: sincere in the zahir preservation, but having bypassed the nass-designated bāṭin succession. The māhiyya of Islamic community governance was preserved; the iḍāfa to the Imamic succession chain was cut (F-01 Locked Formula applied to the founding succession event).

Part VI

The Anti-Sectarian Frame — Why This Is Quranic Structural Analysis, Not Partisan Claim

The standard objection to the Shia reading of Saqīfa is that it is a partisan sectarian claim — a minority community grieving its political defeat and constructing a theological narrative to retroactively justify that grievance. This objection has structural force when the Saqīfa argument is presented purely as "ʿAlī should have been caliph." It collapses entirely when the argument is presented as Quranic structural analysis.

The SCRA's anti-sectarian framing has three moves:

Move 1: The question is not "Shia or Sunni" but "does the Hārūn pattern apply?" This is a Quranic structural question. Does the Ghadīr Khumm declaration constitute a nass in the Hārūn-pattern sense? Does the Saqīfa event map onto the structural template the Quran documented? Any Muslim who accepts the Quran's authority must take this structural question seriously — it cannot be dismissed as Shia sectarianism without dismissing the Quranic structural template itself.

Move 2: The Banī Isrāʾīl comparison is not an insult — it is the Quran's own structural analysis. The Quran compares the Islamic community to the Banī Isrāʾīl repeatedly as a warning: Q 3:65 (O People of the Book, why do you dispute about Ibrāhīm when the Torah and Gospel were not sent down until after him?); Q 2:40-74 (extensive Banī Isrāʾīl address); Q 5:12-13 (God's covenant with the Banī Isrāʾīl and their breaking of it). The Quran's documentation of the Banī Isrāʾīl's structural errors is a pre-emptive warning to the Islamic community: recognize these patterns when they appear in your own history. Applying the Hārūn-pattern analysis to Saqīfa is following the Quran's own hermeneutic instruction.

Move 3: The nass principle is theologically neutral — it either applies or it doesn't. If nass is the Furqān criterion for prophetic succession (which the entire prophetic chain confirms), then the question is simply: was Ghadīr a nass? If it was, the Hārūn pattern applies to Saqīfa. If it was not, then a new argument must explain why the structural template that governed all previous prophetic succession did not apply in the Islamic succession. The absence of a principled explanation for this exception is itself structurally significant.

SCRA Position on the Sectarian Question The SCRA is not a partisan Shia institution in the sense of being committed to intra-Muslim political conflict. It is committed to structural accuracy. The Hārūn-pattern analysis produces a structural conclusion: Saqīfa was a Quranic structural event — the recurrence of the Banī Isrāʾīl pattern in the Islamic community. This conclusion is not anti-Sunni. The early caliphs were not malicious agents — they were sincere Muslims who made a structural error under enormous pressure, at an unprecedented moment, in ways the Quran's structural analysis had forecast. The error's consequences — fourteen centuries of Islamic civilizational fragmentation, Ba'alist penetration of Islamic institutions, the suppression of the Imamic succession line — are structural, not personal. Recognizing the structural error does not require condemning the individuals who made it; it requires understanding the civilizational trajectory their choice initiated.
Part VII

Imam ʿAlī's Own Account — Nahj al-Balāgha as Primary Source

The most important primary source for the Saqīfa structural analysis is Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) himself — the wasī whose appointment was bypassed. His account in Nahj al-Balāgha is the most authoritative, most immediate, and most structurally precise description of what happened and why.

Nahj al-Balāgha Sermon 3 — Khutba al-Shiqshiqiyya (The Sermon of the Camel's Groan)

Imam ʿAlī (A.S.): "By God, the son of Abī Quḥāfa [Abū Bakr] dressed himself in it [the caliphate] while he well knew that my position in relation to it was the same as the position of the axis in relation to the hand-mill. The flood water flows down from me and the bird cannot fly up to me. I put a curtain against the caliphate and kept myself detached from it."

"Then I began to think whether I should assault or endure calmly the blinding darkness of tribulations wherein the grown-up are made feeble and the young grow old and the true believer acts under strain till he meets God. I found that endurance thereon was wiser. So I adopted patience although there was a pricking in the eye and suffocation [as if something were stuck] in the throat. I watched the plundering of my inheritance till the first one went his way but handed over the caliphate to Ibn al-Khaṭṭāb after himself..."

The Imam's self-restraint reasoning: "I therefore withheld my hand till I saw that many people were reverting from Islam and trying to destroy the religion of Muḥammad... I therefore feared that if I did not protect Islam and its people, and I were to see in it a breach or destruction, it would mean a greater calamity to me than the loss of authority over you."

This passage is the Shia theology's most important primary source because it establishes three things simultaneously: (1) The Imam explicitly identifies his appointment as legitimate and the succession as unjust; (2) He explains his strategic self-restraint — protecting Islamic community cohesion took precedence over enforcing his appointment by force; (3) He frames his patience in terms that are precisely parallel to Hārūn's response to Mūsā (Q 7:150): the wasī restrains himself to prevent community fragmentation.

Nahj al-Balāgha Letter 62 contains another crucial structural statement from Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) — his letter explaining the Saqīfa events to the people of Egypt: "The people rushed to Abū Bakr and pledged allegiance to him. I withheld my hand till I saw people reverting from Islam... The matter of the caliphate is not a personal one for me; it is the matter of the community's survival on the correct path." The Imam frames the succession not as personal grievance but as civilizational trajectory — exactly the SCRA structural analysis.

Part VIII

Civilizational Consequences — Fourteen Centuries of Structural Derivation

The Saqīfa event is the SCRA's foundational explanation for fourteen centuries of Islamic civilizational fragmentation, Ba'alist penetration of Islamic institutions, and the continuous tension between the zahir-caliphate/sultanate structures and the bāṭin-walāya tradition preserved through the Imamic succession and the Sufi silsilas.

The structural consequences cascade from the Saqīfa decision in a precise pattern:

Immediate (first generation): The Imamic succession is severed from state power. Imam ʿAlī (A.S.) governs for only four years (35–40 AH) of the first three decades of Islamic state history. The community's governance is organized around zahir-caliphate structures rather than the nass-based walāya-succession. The Fadakiyya confrontation (Fatima al-Zahrāʾ's (A.S.) challenge to the caliphate's first major decision) establishes the Furqān-criterion test that the new caliphate cannot pass (see WP-15).

First century: The Umayyad restoration. The Quraysh commercial oligarchy that the Prophet (ﷺ) had dismantled reasserts itself through Muʿāwiya's political maneuvering and Yazīd's succession. Karbala (61 AH/680 CE) is the direct consequence: the Ba'alist Umayyad structure, now controlling the caliphate zahir, moves to physically eliminate the Imamic bāṭin succession line. Imam Ḥusayn (A.S.)'s shahādat is the structural confirmation that the Saqīfa decision had created the conditions for Ba'alist capture of the Islamic state.

Subsequent centuries: The Abbasid extraction (using Alid symbolic legitimacy to seize power, then suppressing Alid substance — WP: abbasid-extraction-mechanism); the Mihna (theological capture attempt — WP: mihna-mutazila); the Sufi silsila tradition as the bāṭin transmission mechanism operating under Ba'alist hegemony. Every major Islamic civilizational struggle in subsequent centuries can be traced structurally to the foundational succession question: whose authority is legitimate, and on what basis?

SCRA Analytical Verdict — WP-82

The Saqīfa event is not a Shia sectarian grievance retroactively theologized. It is a Quranic structural event — the recurrence of the Hārūn pattern within the Islamic community, occurring exactly as the Quran's structural documentation of that pattern forecast it would. The nass principle (divine appointment as the Furqān criterion for succession) was bypassed at Saqīfa in favor of the constructed substitute authority mechanism (Sāmirī pattern). The civilizational consequences of this bypass — fourteen centuries of Islamic fragmentation, Umayyad restoration, Ba'alist institutional penetration, and the suppression of the Imamic bāṭin succession line — are the structural derivation of the founding succession error.

The anti-sectarian force of this analysis is that it applies the Quran's own structural hermeneutic to the Quran's most important community: the Islamic Umma. The Quran warns the Islamic community against repeating the Banī Isrāʾīl pattern. The SCRA documents that the Umma repeated it — not from malice but from the structural pressure of the moment. The structural error's recognition is not a matter of sectarian partisanship; it is a matter of civilizational accuracy required for understanding why Sacred Civilization (Islamic Civilization = True Umma) has been operating in bāṭin-transmission mode rather than state-power mode for fourteen centuries, and what conditions are required for its restoration.

SCRA Position: Saqīfa = the Hārūn pattern's recurrence in Islamic history. The nass principle = the Furqān criterion for legitimate authority that was bypassed. The subsequent Islamic civilizational trajectory = the structural consequence of this bypass. The Imam Mahdī's (A.T.F.S.) restoration = the structural correction of the Saqīfa diversion at the eschatological moment when conditions permit full Sacred Civilization assertion.
