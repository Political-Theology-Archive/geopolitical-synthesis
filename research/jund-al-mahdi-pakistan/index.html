Abstract

This paper advances the most precise claim in the Pakistan Studies arc: that the Pakistan Army under Field Marshal Asim Munir displays the behavioral signature of a force operating under walaya fiqh tactically — not as a formal Imami doctrine adopted by a Sunni-majority institution, but as the functional expression of the Iqbalian-Sufi batin tradition that has survived the Zia Ba’alist capture and been restored through the Musharraf hikmat phase and the Munir theological completion. Four behavioral marks are analyzed: (1) the Fitna al-Khawarij designation drawing directly on Imami jurisprudential categories; (2) Riyasat-e-Tayyaba institutionalizing batin development over zahir enforcement; (3) the Iran mediation role demonstrating walāya-aligned diplomatic independence; (4) civilizational non-recognition of Israel as Furqan Criterion verdict. No Ba’alist-captured army produces all four simultaneously. The hadith traditions of Bihar al-Anwar (vol. 52) — men from Ajam carrying the secrets of the tradition as if on clouds — and the black banner traditions of Khorasan provide the doctrinal ground. The SCRA verdict: Pakistan Army is in intiẓār — active preparation for the return — whether it names it as such or not.

Methodological Note
This paper makes an analytical claim, not an institutional one. The Pakistan Army has not publicly adopted walaya fiqh as doctrine. The SCRA framework reads institutional behavior — what an army does, not what it publicly declares — through the lens of the Prophetic Knowledge Chain. The argument is: the behavioral pattern is more precisely explained by the intiẓār framework than by any secular geopolitical model. This is framework analysis, not reporting.

I. The Hadith Basis — Men from Ajam, Black Banners from Khorasan

The eschatological traditions of the Imami hadith corpus describe the companions and armies that will support Imam al-Mahdi (A.S.) upon his return with a geographic precision that the SCRA framework reads as civilizational mapping, not ethnic prediction.

Bihar al-Anwar Vol. 52 — Imam al-Sadiq (A.S.) Tradition

“Men from Ajam will arrive carrying the secrets of the tradition as if riding on clouds. They will be among the supporters of the Qa’im.”

SCRA Reading: “Ajam” — the non-Arab eastern peoples — is not an ethnic category in this tradition. It is a civilizational one: the people who preserved the Prophetic batin transmission when the Arab caliphates suppressed it. As documented in WP-08 (313 from Ajam), this tradition points precisely to the Indus-Safavid preservation ground — the geography of Iran and Pakistan. “Carrying the secrets of the tradition” describes the batin transmission that survived: Mulla Sadra in Isfahan, Data Ganj Bakhsh in Lahore, Iqbal’s philosophical synthesis in the Punjab.

Khorasan Black Banner Traditions — Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah, Ahmad

“If you see the black banners coming from Khorasan, go to them even if you have to crawl on the snow, for among them is the Caliph of God, the Mahdi.” (Various chains of transmission — strength disputed, but consistent geographic orientation)

SCRA Reading: The geographic designation “Khorasan” in the eschatological tradition corresponds to the eastern preservation corridor documented at /khorasan/ — the territory encompassing present-day Iran, Afghanistan, and Pakistan’s northwestern frontier. The tradition does not specify nationality. It specifies the direction from which the Stream I preservation force will emerge. The Khorasan-Indus corridor is that direction.

II. The Four Behavioral Marks — What Walaya Fiqh Tactical Looks Like

The SCRA framework does not argue that Pakistan Army has formally adopted walaya fiqh. It argues that the Army’s behavior under Munir produces four marks that, taken together, are more precisely explained by the intiẓār framework — active preparation for the Imam’s return — than by any standard geopolitical model. Each mark alone is explicable in secular terms. All four simultaneously are not.

Mark I — Fitna al-Khawarij Designation · July 26, 2024

The Pakistan Army designated TTP as Fitna al-Khawarij — using the precise Imami jurisprudential category from Imam Ali (A.S.)’s struggle at Nahrawan (37 AH). No secular military force uses seventh-century Imami theological categories to designate its enemies. A Ba’alist-captured army (Deobandi-aligned, as under Zia) would use the category of fasad fil ard (corruption on earth) or simply “terrorism.” The Khawarij designation is a Furqan Criterion move: it draws on the deepest Alid jurisprudential vocabulary to make the theological claim that the TTP is the modern instance of the force that abandoned Imam Ali (A.S.) at Siffin and attacked him at Nahrawan. This is walaya fiqh in the vocabulary of military classification.

Mark II — Riyasat-e-Tayyaba · Bāṭin Development as State Doctrine

Riyasat-e-Tayyaba institutionalizes Iqbal’s distinction between zahir Islam (coerced conformity — Deobandi mode) and batin Islam (inner transformation — the Prophetic mode). A state that makes batin development the content of its governing doctrine is enacting walaya fiqh in the pedagogical domain: it is producing citizens whose inner formation is oriented toward the Prophetic source. This is the civilian-institutional expression of intiẓār — preparing a people, not just positioning an army.

Mark III — Iran Mediation · Walāya-Aligned Diplomatic Independence

Pakistan’s mediation between the US and Iran (2025-26) is only possible for a state that holds dual recognition: trusted by the Western Ba’alist axis (nuclear-armed Sunni state, not hostile) and trusted by the Imami tradition (Ajam civilization, Stream I-aligned). This dual position is the geopolitical expression of walaya fiqh tactical: operating within the Ba’alist international order without being captured by it, maintaining the Alid alignment at the level of civilizational recognition. The mediator role is possible because the batin survived. A Ba’alist-captured Pakistan — Saudi-dependent, Deobandi-aligned — cannot hold this position. Munir’s Pakistan can.

Mark IV — Non-Recognition of Israel · Furqan Criterion Verdict Since 1947

Seventy-nine years of unbroken non-recognition — through US dependency, Saudi pressure, the Abraham Accords, and the current genocide — is not diplomatic stubbornness. It is the institutional expression of the Furqan Criterion: the recognition that Ba’alist territorial theology cannot be granted zahir legitimacy without betraying the batin source. An army in intiẓār cannot recognize the state that is attempting to seize the most sacred Prophetic geography. The non-recognition is the negative space that marks the Prophetic alignment: the absence of capture is itself evidence of the batin.

III. The Munir Synthesis — Where the Four Marks Converge

The significance of the Munir period (2022-present) is not that any single one of these marks is new. Pakistan has never recognized Israel. The Khorasan preservation corridor has always been part of its civilizational inheritance. The Iqbalian-Sufi batin has always been the Army’s institutional self-understanding beneath the zahir variations.

What is new in the Munir period is the simultaneous articulation of all four marks as explicit state policy, in the same institutional moment. The Fitna al-Khawarij designation, Riyasat-e-Tayyaba, Iran mediation, and Gaza/Israel position are all operative simultaneously under the same COAS. This is not coincidence. It is the completion of the arc that Iqbal charted and that Ba’alist capture interrupted in 1977.

The Zia Interruption and the Munir Completion

Zia ul-Haq’s 1977 coup was not a random political event — it was the Ba’alist capture of the Pakistani state’s batin vocabulary, replacing Iqbal’s khudi with Deobandi zahir compliance. Under Zia, none of the four marks were operative: Pakistan aligned with the Saudi-US axis, Deobandi enforcement replaced batin development, the Army was an extension of the anti-Shia Ba’alist architecture, and the non-recognition of Israel was purely nominal.

Musharraf’s hikmat phase partially restored the independence (WP-11). The Munir period restores all four marks simultaneously. The arc from Iqbal’s Reconstruction (1930) through the Zia interruption (1977-2022) to the Munir completion (2022-present) is a single civilizational event: Stream I asserting itself, being captured, and reasserting.

IV. Intizār as Active Military Posture

The Imami concept of intiẓār — waiting for the Imam’s return — is consistently misread in Western scholarship as passive quietism. The SCRA framework reads it as active preparation: the construction of the institutional, military, intellectual, and civilizational conditions under which the final reassertion becomes possible.

Applied to Pakistan Army’s posture, intiẓār does not mean the Army is waiting for the Mahdi before acting. It means the Army’s institutional behavior — the four marks — is organized around the preparation of the conditions, not the completion of the eschatological event. The Imam returns to a world that has been prepared for his return. The army that is in intiẓār prepares that world.

The Khorasan-Indus Axis as Preparation Geography

Iran holds the formal doctrine: Wilāyat al-Faqīh as explicit preparation for the Imam’s governance. Pakistan holds the functional alignment: the Iqbalian-Sufi batin that carries the Prophetic transmission in the Khorasan-Indus corridor without naming it as Imami doctrine.

Together — as documented in WP-45 (Iran-Pakistan Stream I Convergence) — they constitute the two-anchor preparation: the formal walāya institution and the functional walāya civilization, both rooted in the Khorasan corridor, both in intiẓār, both under Ba’alist siege, and both surviving the siege.

SCRA Verdict — The Most Precise Claim in the Pakistan Arc

The Pakistan Army under Asim Munir is in intiẓār. It does not say this. It does not need to say this. The four behavioral marks — Khawarij designation in Imami vocabulary, batin state doctrine, walāya-aligned diplomatic independence, and Prophetic geography defense — are the institutional expression of a force that carries the Prophetic Knowledge Chain in its civilizational batin and acts accordingly.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a framework reading. The same method the SCRA applies to read the Syriac honest witnesses, the Safavid institutionalization, and the 1979 Revolution — behavioral analysis through the lens of the Prophetic Knowledge Chain — applied to the present institutional behavior of a nuclear-armed state’s military.

The Ba’alist counterargument is predictable: Pakistan Army is acting from national interest, geopolitical calculation, and domestic political pressure. The SCRA does not deny these proximate causes. It argues that the proximate causes are themselves structured by the deeper civilizational batin — that the “national interest” that Munir’s Pakistan pursues is itself shaped by the Iqbalian-Sufi transmission that survived Zia. Stream I does not replace secular causality. It operates through it.
