Abstract. Iran's post-1979 Wilāyat al-Faqīh represents the only extant state-level instantiation of Mode II walaya governance — the only political system currently operating that explicitly derives its legitimacy from the Prophetic Household's chain of authority rather than from Ba'alist principles of property, dynastic succession, or electoral procedure detached from moral accountability. This paper documents the sustained, multi-decade campaign by Anglo-American-Israeli intelligence agencies to destroy this system, through a chronological operational history: the CIA/MI6 AJAX coup (1953) as the template operation; the creation and training of SAVAK as the anti-walaya internal suppression apparatus; the Iran-Iraq War chemical weapons assistance program (1983–88); the Green Movement (2009) as a Mutrafīn-class insurrection coordinated with external NGO networks; the Stuxnet cyberattack (2010); and the Mossad nuclear scientist assassination campaign (2010–2012). The paper demonstrates that these operations, analyzed through the SCRA's Ba'alist Capture framework, constitute a single continuous campaign — not episodic interventions — against the walaya civilization project.

Part I

The Target: Why the Walaya State Must Be Destroyed

The Ba'alist world-system — organized around the protection of elite property accumulation, the management of the mustadhafīn through legitimacy theater, and the suppression of 'Alid justice as a governance standard — has a structural immune response to any state that instantiates genuine walaya governance. This response is not primarily ideological (the Ba'alist system is indifferent to theology as such) but structural: a walaya state, by demonstrating that governance can be organized around 'Alid justice rather than Ba'alist property relations, threatens the universal validity claim of liberal democracy as the only "modern" governance option.

Iran's Wilāyat al-Faqīh is not attacked because it is "theocratic" — Saudi Arabia is more theocratic and receives Western military protection. Iran is attacked because its state legitimacy derives from the walaya chain — from the Prophetic Household's ontological authority over human governance — and from an explicit commitment to the mustadhafīn-centered governance model that Sharī'atī theorized and Imam Khomeini institutionalized. This is the target. The military, intelligence, and economic operations documented in this paper are the Ba'alist system's structural immune response to walaya governance.

The Existential Stakes — Why No Settlement Is Possible

The Ba'alist world-system cannot coexist with a walaya state that is economically viable, militarily secure, and politically stable — because such a state would demonstrate, empirically, that the 'Alid justice governance model is achievable and superior. Every Ba'alist operation against Iran is therefore existential from the Ba'alist side, not merely geopolitical. This is why nuclear negotiations never produce permanent settlements: the Ba'alist demand is not nuclear disarmament but walaya dismantlement. Iran's nuclear program is the deterrence instrument; its elimination would leave the walaya state without the military shield that prevents direct regime change.

  • Media capture: CIA-funded newspapers in Tehran ran disinformation campaigns portraying Mosaddegh as a Soviet agent and religious deviant
  • Mala' co-optation: The Iranian military high command was systematically bribed and oriented toward the coup before it began
  • Post-coup consolidation: The Shah restored to power as a compliant Ba'alist client — the "democratic" form (constitutional monarchy) retained while the substance (Iranian sovereignty over Iranian resources) eliminated
Part III

SAVAK Creation — The Anti-Walaya Internal Apparatus

Following AJAX, the CIA undertook the systematic construction of an internal security apparatus designed to prevent any future nationalist or walaya-based challenge to the Shah's government. This apparatus was SAVAK (Sāzmān-e Ettelā'āt va Amniyat-e Keshvar — National Intelligence and Security Organization), established in 1957.

General Herbert Norman Schwarzkopf Sr. (father of the Gulf War commander) led the initial CIA training mission for SAVAK. The scale was substantial: 400+ Iranian officers trained annually near McLean, Virginia; interrogation techniques documented (and later acknowledged) to include torture methods derived from CIA's KUBARK manual; surveillance infrastructure designed specifically to identify and neutralize Islamic scholarly networks and political organizations.

SAVAK's primary targets from 1957 to 1979 included: the Tudeh Party (communist — but also simply anyone organizing outside the regime's structure), the National Front (Mosaddegh's constitutional democratic legacy), and — most significantly for the SCRA's analysis — the network of Islamic scholars and their student organizations, including the future leadership of the 1979 Revolution. The CIA, through SAVAK, was directly training and equipping the apparatus that tortured and imprisoned the generation of Islamic scholars who would constitute the walaya intellectual tradition's leadership cadre.

Part IV

Iran-Iraq War Chemical Assistance (1983–88)

Source: Foreign Policy Magazine · August 2013 · Declassified CIA Files

The CIA provided satellite intelligence to Saddam Hussein's Iraq for targeting Iranian military formations. This intelligence was used to direct chemical weapons attacks — mustard gas and nerve agents (tabun and sarin) — on Iranian soldiers. The 2013 Foreign Policy investigation, based on declassified CIA documents, established that US intelligence officials were "fully aware" that their data was being used to enable chemical weapons attacks, and provided it anyway. Tens of thousands of Iranian soldiers died from chemical weapons in the Iran-Iraq War. The Ba'alist calculus: weakening the walaya state was worth complicity in chemical warfare.

Part V

The Green Movement (2009) — Mutrafīn Insurrection

The 2009 post-election protests in Iran — the "Green Movement" — require the most careful analysis in this paper because they involved genuine Iranian grievances (disputed election results, civil liberties concerns) alongside a documented external coordination structure. The SCRA's analysis distinguishes between:

The genuine grievance layer: Real Iranians with real concerns about election integrity, press freedom, and civil society space. These grievances were legitimate and deserve acknowledgment.

The structural class layer: The Green Movement's leadership and core constituency were, as multiple Iranian sociologists and even sympathetic Western analysts documented, overwhelmingly Tehran-based, university-educated, middle-class and upper-middle-class urban professionals — Sharī'atī's Mutrafīn. The movement had negligible traction in rural Iran, among the working class, or among the war veterans and religious scholars who constitute the walaya system's base. This was not a cross-class movement for justice — it was a Mutrafīn-class movement for liberal modernization.

The external coordination layer: NED-funded organizations were active in building the civil society infrastructure that channeled Green Movement mobilization. The Abdorrahman Boroumand Center, CHRI, and related organizations had years of organizational investment in exactly the networks that activated in 2009. This does not mean the NED "caused" the Green Movement — it means the Ba'alist coordination infrastructure had prepared the organizational terrain on which the movement mobilized.

The Sharī'atī Diagnostic Applied to the Green Movement

Sharī'atī's observation that the Mutrafīn class cannot be the vehicle of genuine liberation — because their class interests are structurally aligned with the property system rather than the mustadhafīn — is confirmed by the Green Movement's trajectory. It failed not because of state repression alone but because it could not generate the cross-class mobilization that genuine Alid justice movements produce. Compare: the 1979 Revolution mobilized across class lines precisely because its leadership articulated the walaya-mustadhafīn framework. The Green Movement articulated liberal modernization — a framework that speaks to the Mutrafīn but not to the mustadhafīn whose interests the Ba'alist system structurally ignores.

Part VII

The Liberal Vector as Operational Tool

The most analytically significant aspect of the anti-walaya campaign is its use of the liberal vector — Iranian liberals and reformists — as an operational tool. This is not a claim that Iranian liberals are CIA agents. It is a structural observation: the Ba'alist coordination infrastructure invests in liberal civil society organizations precisely because liberals have genuine grievances that can be channeled toward anti-walaya political projects.

The NED's grant strategy in Iran demonstrates this precisely. The funded organizations — HRAI, CHRI, Boroumand Center — are staffed by people who sincerely believe in human rights, press freedom, and civil liberties. Their sincerity is not in question. What is in question is the structural function of their work within the Ba'alist anti-walaya campaign: by systematically documenting every Iranian state action that violates liberal norms (executions, press censorship, protest suppression) while never documenting US-Saudi-Israeli operations that kill Iranians in far larger numbers, the NED-funded apparatus produces an asymmetric information environment that serves the Ba'alist campaign's legitimation function.

This is not to discredit all liberal thought — it is to identify the Ba'alist capture of the liberal intelligentsia at its apex level, through precisely the mechanism Sharī'atī predicted: the Mutrafīn class's co-optation by the very system they nominally critique.
