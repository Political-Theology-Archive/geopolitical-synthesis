WP-27 (Hakimiyya Capture) implies it is Instance VI but does not formally enumerate it as such. A dedicated paper OR a revision to WP-21 (Caliphate Capture Chain) should add this as the sixth entry in the taxonomy. The Chain paper (WP-21) ends at 1924 — it needs a sixth entry for the Pseudo-Islamic Vector.

### 2. Iran-Pakistan Theological Comparison Needs Depth
WP-45 is a thin stub. The actual theological comparison between Iran's *wilayat al-faqih* and Pakistan's Sufi silsila structure as parallel expressions of walayah needs a full analytical paper. This is one of the most important civilizational arguments in the corpus.

### 3. The Three Registers of Crypto-Alid Transmission
WP-51 identifies the du'a form as encrypted transmission. WP-20 identifies poetry as encrypted transmission. WP-15 identifies the al-Ghazali route as encrypted transmission. A synthesizing paper on Crypto-Alid transmission as a systematic mechanism would complete this strand.

### 4. The Contemporary Ba'alist Node (Zionism/Third Temple)
WP-44 and the Third Temple standalone study address this but from different angles. The Ba'alist territorial theology applied to contemporary Israel needs fuller development — it is a significant arc in the contemporary reckoning.

## IDEAS FROM AUTHOR'S INTELLIGENCE / CONCERNS

Patterns observed in the author's research choices that indicate their areas of deep concern:

1. **The Pakistan Army as civilizational actor** — The author is not a naive supporter but a sophisticated analyst who sees the army's *institutional* civilizational role as distinct from individual officers or political phases. This needs careful maintenance — never flatten to either "army bad" or "army good."

2. **Bhutto as tragic figure** — The author's emotional and intellectual investment in Bhutto is significant. His assassination is framed as the Ba'alist Capture's defining act. Future papers should honor this framing.

3. **Cross-confessional methodology** — The author consistently uses Sunni primary sources (Bukhari, Tirmidhi, Ahmad ibn Hanbal) to establish points that are primarily Shia in their implications. This is the academic move that makes the corpus credible to a broad scholarly audience. Maintain this.

4. **The Indus Basin as sacred geography** — The author has a deep personal connection to the Indus-Pakistan sacred landscape (IVC, Sufi shrines, dargahs). Papers on this geography should be written with reverence, not just analysis.

5. **Eschatological dimension** — WP-46 (Jund al-Mahdi) and WP-49 (Khutba al-Gharra') show the author takes the eschatological dimension of Islamic political theology seriously. This is not symbolic — it is meant literally within the tradition's framework.
